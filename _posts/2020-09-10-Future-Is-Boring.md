## Hello, Google

![_config.yml]({{ site.baseurl }}/images/trek/scotty_hello_computer.gif)

The perfectly intuitive computer interface is a very, very old trope. Isaac Asimov's stories depicted near-future computers machines you could type plain English into a computer and it would respond in like. distant future computers were portrayed as anthropomorphic albeit emotionless and logic beings. Check out [The Last Question](https://templatetraining.princeton.edu/sites/training/files/the_last_question_-_issac_asimov.pdf) for a great short story that emphasizes this. Sci-fi is full of computers you could just type into or talk at and it would respond with incredible accuracy and understanding. This ranges from the sily, like [Adam West's Batman pouring alphabet soup into a computer](https://www.imdb.com/title/tt0519458/) to the typically more plausible voice interface of Star Trek computers.

> I think it's blown a bat-fuse - all those noodle letters.

For the most part, the computers from Star Trek are a reality now. In the original series episode [Wolf in the Fold](https://memory-alpha.fandom.com/wiki/Wolf_in_the_Fold_(episode)) for example, the Enterprise computer was tasked with doing a rather complex database query to find unsolved serial killers across the known galaxy and it found exactly what Spock needed to see a trail of deaths including London's Jack the Ripper and culminating with the being they'd captured. Later, Spock ties up the computer by giving it an impossible numerical computation task. Superficially, this seems as implausible as pouring soup into the bat-computer, but in the context of modern technology it's quite reasonable.

> Computer, this is a class A compulsory directive. Compute to the last digit, the value of pi.

- The crew speaks at the computer in fairly plain English, though making a point to use simple sentence structures and enunciating clearly. they even precede commands with a "wake word" not unlike "Hey Siri".

- In other episodes it is established that each Federation ship carriers an enormous database of general information including extensive historical records. It seems perfectly likely that there would be records in this on-ship database would include mention of notable unsolved mysteries that caught significant public attention across the Federation worlds, including more digested forms a la Wikipedia articles. It's already easy enough to download the entirety of Wikipedia's text to a consumer PC, so a Federation starship should be capable of carrying orders of magnitude more. This turns the serial killer search into a plausible datamining operation not much more complex than a typical Google search. Searching Bing for "unsolved serial killers in the USA" immediately delivers 198,000 results, with a [Wikipedia category](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Category:Unidentified_serial_killers) among the first few results.

- Spock's computational directive is a bit trite (how many evil AIs have been stopped this way?) but absolutely plausible. [Wolfram Alpha](https://www.wolframalpha.com/input/?i=Compute+to+the+last+digit%2C+the+value+of+pi) is not only intelligent enough to parse Spock's directive but clever enough to note that pi is irrational and has no last digit.

The Star Trek computers and their usage (usually) remain plausible going forward through the movies, Next Generation, Deep Space 9, and even Voyager, the series where [going too fast made two character "hyper-evolve" into salamanders](https://memory-alpha.fandom.com/wiki/Threshold_(episode)) that reproduced before they were able to be de-evolved somehow by destroying their DNA with antimatter. Nobody ever accused Star Trek of being especially realistic, at least in terms of science. Regardless, most commands to the computers are straight-forward and use simpler sentence structures. Most characters enunciate more clearly when commanding a computer. Modern speech recognition systems are more than capable of understanding the sorts of commands issued to the computers of Star Trek. Commands as simple as "tea, Earl Grey, hot" or "coffee, black" or even "reduce the lighting by 15%" are simple compared to the sorts of complex implicit syntax contemporary systems are designed to handle.

### So Where Is It?

Computers are technically capable of understanding me when I say "tea, Earl Grey, hot", and I've got a digital coffee maker capable of loading tea and brewing a cup. The technology is here, so why can't I order a computer in my home to make me some "tea, Earl Grey, hot?" The answer is marketing and business strategy.

A common philosophy in marketing is that you want to present a product that solves a specific problem customers have, or improves something that's already good enough. This could well require convincing customers they have a problem that doesn't exist, or that your product improves something that it really doesn't. Enter the contemporary digital assistant, the closest thing we have today to Star Trek's computers. I can talk to Cortana and ask it to tell me a joke. I can ask Siri to reccomend a good restaurant for pho in my area. I can tell Google to add a reminder to my calendar. This can all be quite useful, and these services address specific tasks that many people need performed.

What I can't do is anything practical. I can't tell Cortana to shutdown my PC or even open a specific file or run a specific application. I can't ask Google where my keys are despite the Google Home cameras having seen where I put them and the object recognition intelligence necessary for finding my keys being well within the abilities of a mid-range smartphone. I can't tell Siri to open the app that controls my coffee makers, select Earl Grey, and brew me a cup of hot tea. These things are all technically possible and within the ability of even a hobbyist to implement for themselves, but off-the-shelf these systems not only lack general utility but require jumping through some weird hoops to add utility. More complex things, like querying multiple retailers and finding the lowest price for a specific product, require far fewer hoops, hacks, and work-arounds to work.

It makes sense from a business marketing perspective. These services are ultimately money-making operations, so during their development a lot of effort went into identifying what customers would be most interested in. It's hard to convince someone to buy a $500 gadget that will do whatever they ask and expect them to figure out what they should ask. Early home PCs tried this and market demand was minimal until manufacturers started advertising specific things a PC could be used for. It has proven much easier to convince customers to pay $500 or more on a gadget that does specific things off a list, even if those things aren't as useful as other things might be.

## Where We're Going We Don't Need Roads

![_config.yml]({{ site.baseurl }}/images/technology/flying-car.png)

The flying car. Right up there with androids and teleporters as trite, impossible sci-fi technology, right? Not so much. Flying cars are real, have been real in some capacity for decades, and you can just go out and buy one today. 

### A Brief History of Flying Cars

If you want to be annoying, point out that the basic criteria of a flying car had been met since helicopters arrived on the consumer market. They're expensive, loud, pricey, inefficient, not cheap, require designated landing pads, and cost an arm and a leg. Granted, this isn't the ideal most people have in mind when they think of flying cars, but it fits. Maybe you want something more flexible, able to drive on the street when it isn't flying. [Roadable aircraft](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roadable_aircraft) have existed for over 80 years now. Drive to an airport, attach your wings, fly to the next town's airport, land, take off the wings, and drive the rest of the way. The problem is a simple matter of supply and demand. Not many people are willing to get a pilot's license and pay a small fortune for a machine that's at best a mediocre airplane and mediocre car. Those that could afford it are more likely to buy a decent car and a decent plane for around the same price. Small demand means small production and small production means high prices. Today, there are several companies selling or developing more practical flying machines that could fit your vision of a flying car. Most of these are leveraging the recent rise of cheap quadcopters and other multi-rotor drones. Multiple ducted fans or even just a ton of small naked propellers provide stable lift sufficient to carry a person. But most of these are being developed by small startups or companies not heavily involved in the car or aircraft business. Why aren't companies like Ford or Cessna getting into the flying car business?

### So Where Is It?

Early automobiles were dangerous. Compared to a horse, running into something in a motorized horseless carriage was not only easy to do by mistake but also a lot more dangerous. Early automobile manufacturers waged an uphill battle against public opinion, as the idea of trading in your horse for a tempermental machine that needed just as much maintenance and was significantly more dangerous was unsurprisingly unpopular. The driving forces for automobiles were the rise of post-industrial urban centers and the transition away from farms and into city housing. Cars are still exceptionally dangerous compared to almost any other form of travel, although safer than they used to be. They're still at least as expensive as a horse once you factor in all the expenses we're accustomed to, from gasoline and garages and driveways and repairs and license fees to road taxes and oil subsidies. Many people like to drive but nobody likes sitting in traffic, a thing that didn't really exist prior to the restructuring of society around cars. While cars aren't something we can really opt out of today, they weren't necessarly an improvement in most regards. If you're traveling out of town or in severe heat or cold, cars are definitely an improvement. They're more convenient than horses, certainly, given the convneience of parking them and elaving them for days at a time.  For all their shortcomings, there are definite benefits to cars that made them sensible to manufacture and sell.

So what about flying cars? They're a lot like early automobiles. They're a lot more dangerous than cars, since an engine failure means crashing and the severity of that crash depends not just on speed but mostly on altitude. Unless you're going to restrict flying cars to flying over roads (which would really defeat the purpose) then there's the issue of what or who you crash down on top of. As a society, kids are started with the concepts of cars as toddlers, steering with toy steering wheels and pushing toy cars along model roads. The first few generations of flying car owners would be learning from scratch. It's probably safe to say that the risks and dangers associated with the first flying cars will be comparable to those associated with the first automobiles, but those worked out so perhaps it could be done again. Or perhaps not. Society has changed a bit since the 19th Century. Every death or injury in a car is a risk that the manufacturer could face a legal battle to prove it wasn't the car that was unsafe. When the first generation of flying cars will most definitely be unsafe, that is a lot of liability for the company to absorb and a lot of waivers and disclaimers for customers to sign.
