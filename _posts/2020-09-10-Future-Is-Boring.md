The Future is here and it's really, really boring. Most of the technology science fiction imagined has come to reality, but it isn't how we wanted it.

## Hello, Google

![_config.yml]({{ site.baseurl }}/images/trek/scotty_hello_computer.gif)

The perfectly intuitive computer interface is a very, very old trope. Isaac Asimov's stories depicted near-future computers machines you could type plain English into a computer and it would respond in like. distant future computers were portrayed as anthropomorphic albeit emotionless and logic beings. Check out [The Last Question](https://templatetraining.princeton.edu/sites/training/files/the_last_question_-_issac_asimov.pdf) for a great short story that emphasizes this. Sci-fi is full of computers you could just type into or talk at and it would respond with incredible accuracy and understanding. This ranges from the sily, like [Adam West's Batman pouring alphabet soup into a computer](https://www.imdb.com/title/tt0519458/) to the typically more plausible voice interface of Star Trek computers.

> I think it's blown a bat-fuse - all those noodle letters.

For the most part, the computers from Star Trek are a reality now. In the original series episode [Wolf in the Fold](https://memory-alpha.fandom.com/wiki/Wolf_in_the_Fold_(episode)) for example, the Enterprise computer was tasked with doing a rather complex database query to find unsolved serial killers across the known galaxy and it found exactly what Spock needed to see a trail of deaths including London's Jack the Ripper and culminating with the being they'd captured. Later, Spock ties up the computer by giving it an impossible numerical computation task. Superficially, this seems as implausible as pouring soup into the bat-computer, but in the context of modern technology it's quite reasonable.

> Computer, this is a class A compulsory directive. Compute to the last digit, the value of pi.

- The crew speaks at the computer in fairly plain English, though making a point to use simple sentence structures and enunciating clearly. they even precede commands with a "wake word" not unlike "Hey Siri".

- In other episodes it is established that each Federation ship carriers an enormous database of general information including extensive historical records. It seems perfectly likely that there would be records in this on-ship database would include mention of notable unsolved mysteries that caught significant public attention across the Federation worlds, including more digested forms a la Wikipedia articles. It's already easy enough to download the entirety of Wikipedia's text to a consumer PC, so a Federation starship should be capable of carrying orders of magnitude more. This turns the serial killer search into a plausible datamining operation not much more complex than a typical Google search. Searching Bing for "unsolved serial killers in the USA" immediately delivers 198,000 results, with a [Wikipedia category](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Category:Unidentified_serial_killers) among the first few results.

- Spock's computational directive is a bit trite (how many evil AIs have been stopped this way?) but absolutely plausible. [Wolfram Alpha](https://www.wolframalpha.com/input/?i=Compute+to+the+last+digit%2C+the+value+of+pi) is not only intelligent enough to parse Spock's directive but clever enough to note that pi is irrational and has no last digit.
