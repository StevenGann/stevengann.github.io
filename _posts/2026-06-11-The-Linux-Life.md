## Introduction

Windows is awful. I know people have been saying that since Windows 1.0 and were saying the same about MS-DOS before that, but I feel like everyone can agree that Windows is an ignoble demonstration of what Cory Doctorow calls "[enshittification](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Enshittification)". My introduction to computers was with Windows 95 and perhaps out of sheer nostalgia I still hold it up as one of the best examples of computer UI/UX. I greatly disliked 98 and ME, but XP felt solid and dependable especially with Service Pack 3. Vista was a mess, but Windows 7 fixed it with convenient features and that sexy Aero Glass effect. From Windows 8 and onward, however, I have found Windows to be just less and less tolerable. Windows 10 became _acceptable_ by the time Windows 11 released, and frankly Windows 11 is just clunky.

I don't want to focus on the negatives of Windows, here. The Internet has more than enough people talking about what they don't like and what is terrible. My point is to illustrate that I cut my teeth on Windows and I am not a hardcore FOSS idealist. If Windows 7 suddenly got modern compatibility and security patches I would have it on all my desktop systems in a heartbeat. That's not happening and I am not keen on running outdated, unpatched, incompatible OSes on systems I actually use for day-to-day work.

## If You Hate Windows So Much, Just Use Linux

I will admit I've been grousing about Windows since I was a teenager. I'd played a bit with [Ubuntu](https://ubuntu.com/), [Knoppix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knoppix), and a few other distros, but always ended up going back to Windows. Maybe I've gotten better at technical problem solving since then, but Linux distros were certainly less stable and less convenient back then. My father, an IT analyst with Microsoft certifications, would get tired of hearing me complain about Windows and say that I should just switch to Linux. To this I would respond with silence or some weak excuse because I had tried several times to use Linux as my main OS. Tried and failed.

## Necessity: The Mother of Adaptation

Two things pushed me toward getting serious about using Linux. The first was frugal parents. In college, I needed a laptop but my parents weren't the kind to drop $2,000 on something that might be obsolete in five years assuming I didn't drop it or lose it before then. I had multiple laptops, all of them scavenged from the e-waste at my father's office. In 2012 my daily driver laptop was a Core2 Duo with 2GB of RAM, and Windows XP was almost too much for it. I installed [Lubuntu](https://lubuntu.me/) on it and marveled at the performance improvements, right up until I realized I didn't have Wi-Fi. For whatever reason, the Broadcom Wi-Fi chipset in that laptop had no driver support in the official repositories. Broadcom themselves offered a Linux driver in the form of a ZIP file full of C source code and no instructions. Needless to say, I never got Wi-Fi working and made a habit of carrying a long Ethernet cable around with me.

The second big motivator was [Raspberry Pi](https://www.raspberrypi.com/). For all its quirks and flaws, the Pi was an amazing platform for prototyping all sorts of projects. It was the hot new thing when I was in grad school so everyone was shoving it into their projects. Of course, running Linux meant there was some complexity and baggage beyond the flash-and-go experience of most microcontroller boards so I had a real motivation to get comfortable with navigating Linux over SSH. After college, I'd bring my experience with Raspberry Pi into my professional work, banging out all sorts of prototypes and glue logic with Raspberry Pis running Python scripts. (Years later, that comfort with a rack of Linux boxes would grow into [a whole homelab](/posts/Homelab-Lessons-1/).)

## The Hold Out and the Last Straw

When Windows 11 released I had intended to stick with the matured Windows 10 until there was a good reason to upgrade. Microsoft apparently wasn't going to tolerate that strategy because when I kept ignoring the daily full-screen popups telling me my PC would soon be unsupported and unprotected, they elected to deploy the "Windows 11 Compatibility Checker" on my system. It was a background process using ~10% of my CPU, over 16GB of RAM, and enough disk and network I/O at all times to cause performance issues with games and media. After every attempt to kill the malware failed, I folded and let it upgrade me to Windows 11. This only made everything worse. My boot time went from ~30 seconds to over 4 minutes, idle CPU usage on my overclocked i9-9900K was over 20%, and idle RAM usage was over 10GB. My Internet seemed slow and I quickly realized why: [Wireshark](https://www.wireshark.org/) revealed Windows 11 was uploading a constant encrypted stream to Microsoft servers at all times, over 5GB per day.

I had held out tolerating Windows for the sake of games alone. I'd long since [given up on Windows for servers](/posts/Homelab-Lessons-2/), so my desktop and laptop were the last Windows systems I used. In a fit of frustration I backed up my local files to [my NAS](/posts/Into-The-Rack/) and installed [Pop!_OS](https://pop.system76.com/) on my PC. Not too long after, I bought a new laptop and chose to save $70 by having Ubuntu preinstalled instead of Windows. After years of talking about it I had finally pulled the trigger and now I was living the Linux life.

But did it solve all my problems?

## The Good

I cannot express how nice the performance improvement with Linux is. My PC, which typically ran a bit hot under Windows, is totally silent until there's a genuine load like a game. Idle CPU and RAM barely register at ~2% CPU and ~1GB RAM used. There are no blasts of mystery data being uploaded anywhere, and the terminal emulators in both Pop!_OS and Ubuntu are much more polished, responsive, and comfortable than CMD or PowerShell on Windows. Installing applications is a breeze, usually through `sudo apt install` or occasionally a [Flatpak](https://flatpak.org/), DEB package, or [AppImage](https://appimage.org/). Reformatting my HDD to ext4 gave it new life with performance more than suitable for disk-heavy games. No more unexpected updates either. One pain point with Windows has always been leaving something running overnight or leaving the system on for remote use later only to have it reboot from a mandatory update. I'd disabled these updates in Windows' settings and even used registry hacks to prevent them, but none of my efforts would stick and Windows continued to be too unreliable for use as a serious workstation. But with Linux, I get a notification that important updates are available and I reboot when I choose to, not when Microsoft chooses.

As for gaming, I had already warmed up to gaming on Linux thanks to my [Steam Deck](https://www.steamdeck.com/), which runs nearly all my games with minimal fuss on at least medium settings if not full quality. More and more games support Linux officially these days, but Valve and the FOSS community have done amazing things with [Wine](https://www.winehq.org/) and [Proton](https://github.com/ValveSoftware/Proton) that make most games run just fine on Linux! I have observed that some older games, from the Vista and XP eras or even older, run better through Proton than they do on Windows 10 or 11.

## The Bad

Nothing is ever perfect and nothing is ever without redeeming qualities. There are a few things I genuinely miss about Windows, even Windows 11. I am very aesthetically oriented and love doing coordinated wallpapers or even animated wallpapers with [Wallpaper Engine](https://www.wallpaperengine.io/). Unfortunately, Wallpaper Engine doesn't support Linux and because of the diversity in Linux desktop configurations there are few good options and fewer still for a less popular distro like Pop!_OS. Pop!_OS has a lot of customization options however, so it isn't too bad. The Ubuntu on my laptop isn't quite as accommodating. Of course the UI is themable if I want to dig deep into the config files, and there are some wallpaper managers that fully support Ubuntu, but it isn't a convenient path and I am trying to avoid fussing and tinkering with my computers more than I want to, and right now my need to customize isn't strong enough to make me bother.

## The Ugly

Linux has a certain reputation among Windows fans for being unstable, buggy, and in constant need of tweaks and fixes. I'd say that was a lot more accurate 10-15 years ago, but modern Linux is still not without its problems. Frankly, nothing on Ubuntu has been a problem to date. The Pop!_OS install on my desktop is another story. I chose Pop!_OS because it is designed expressly for gaming. It has Nvidia GPU drivers as standard, which have historically been a pain to set up. Pop!_OS was specifically intended to be the best Linux distro for gaming.

Unfortunately, my experience has been less than stellar. I tried Pop!_OS a few years ago when I first heard about it but I abandoned it because it didn't feel polished enough to be comfortable all the time. Today, it still feels the same way. The desktop environment is especially buggy, with window sizes snapping back to full screen size when moving the window around. There's a daily issue where the scroll wheel is massively over-sensitive at boot so I have to turn it down and a few hours later the sensitivity drops so I have to turn it back up. I usually use [Flameshot](https://flameshot.org/) on Linux systems and bind it to a hotkey to mimic the behavior of [ShareX](https://getsharex.com/) on Windows systems, but despite a couple hours of fussing with it I wasn't able to bind it to the macro button on my mouse. Most damning of all, some games with first-person controls are just unable to read the mouse movements so the games are unplayable with a mouse. I've tried a number of different Proton kludges and even a shim that forces the game to render through X11 instead of directly to [Wayland](https://wayland.freedesktop.org/), but nothing has worked. These issues don't seem to crop up on Ubuntu, and with the maturing of Linux support for Nvidia GPUs there's a lot less value in Pop!_OS for me.

## The Stragglers

I am still not Windows-free and might never be. I have exactly two programs that I use that I cannot get working on Linux: [Creality Scan](https://www.creality.com/) and [Cricut Design Space](https://cricut.com/en-us/design-space). Creality Scan does run with Wine, but the USB connection becomes intermittent. Cricut Design Space is an utterly abysmal disaster of software and, despite being a mere Electron application wrapping a web app, it must be installed directly and also does some nonsense that causes it to crash with no logs or error messages in Wine. It seems there will always be a begrudging place for a Windows box in my home, or maybe a VM.

## The Verdict

The Linux life is not all cherries and rainbows, but for me it has been absolutely worth the hassle. At the end of the day, any annoyance or problem I experience with Linux pales in comparison to the problems I've had with Windows, and Linux is only improving while Windows seems to be on an accelerating downward spiral.
